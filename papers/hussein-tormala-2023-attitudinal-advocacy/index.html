Attitudinal Advocacy

Mohamed A. Hussein, Zakary L. Tormala

The Cambridge Handbook of Consumer Psychology (2023)

Abstract

On consumer matters, politics, and social issues alike, people regularly advocate on behalf of their attitudes — that is, their opinions or their likes and dislikes. In this chapter, we review recent research on the antecedents of attitudinal advocacy, defined as an expression of support for or opposition to something. We consolidate prior findings into core insights about what drives people to advocate, covering the roles of attitude strength, compensatory motives, perceived efficacy, emotions, attitude framing, and affective versus cognitive bases. We then identify promising directions for future research, including questions about what people say when they advocate, and the advocacy-relevant behaviors of boycotting and censorship that have yet to be formally integrated into the advocacy literature.

Key Findings

  • Attitude strength is the most reliable predictor of advocacy: the more certain and morally convicted people feel about their views, the more likely they are to share, persuade, donate, sign petitions, and take other action on behalf of those views.
  • Doubt can paradoxically increase advocacy through a compensatory mechanism: when people feel uncertain about a core belief, they sometimes advocate more intensely to shore up that belief — a pattern famously observed in doomsday cult members after their prophecy failed.
  • People are more likely to advocate when they believe they can actually make a difference: targeting open-minded audiences (rather than hardened opponents), believing you can make a compelling argument, and expecting your advocacy to produce large attitude shifts all increase willingness to advocate.
  • How attitudes are framed shapes whether people share them: people are more willing to express opinions framed as 'I support X' than 'I oppose Y,' even when the underlying position is identical, because support-framing feels more value-expressive and more likely to win social approval.
  • Message content is shaped by who's doing the advocating: experts write less extreme, less emotional messages than novices; high-power individuals emphasize competence while low-power individuals emphasize warmth; and writing (vs. speaking) produces less emotional advocacy content.
