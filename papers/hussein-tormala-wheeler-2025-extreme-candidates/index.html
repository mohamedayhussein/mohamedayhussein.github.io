Why Do People Choose Extreme Candidates? The Role of Identity Relevance

Mohamed A. Hussein, Zakary L. Tormala, S. Christian Wheeler

Journal of Experimental Social Psychology (2025)

Abstract

Elected officials are increasingly extreme. Research trying to understand this trend has tended to focus on structural factors, such as primary elections and changes in the supply of candidates. Less emphasis has been placed on psychological perspectives. The current research advances such a perspective. Leveraging research on attitudes, we investigate when and why people prefer extreme over moderate candidates from their own party. We posit that the identity relevance of people's attitudes plays a key role. Specifically, we propose that identity relevance fosters attitude extremity, which in turn promotes a preference for extreme over moderate candidates. Across six main studies (N = 3136) using a variety of political issues, operationalizations of identity relevance, instantiations of candidate extremity, and experimental paradigms (including two studies with human-LLM interactions), we find support for this hypothesis. Our findings suggest that as attitudes become more identity relevant, they become more extreme, leading individuals to prefer extreme over moderate candidates from their party.

Key Findings

  • People don't universally prefer extreme candidates — they prefer them when the political issue is personally identity-relevant (i.e., when their stance on it feels like a reflection of who they are). When the issue is not identity-relevant, the preference for extremity disappears or reverses.
  • The causal chain is: identity relevance → attitude extremity → preference for extreme candidates. People adopt more extreme positions on issues they see as self-defining, and those more extreme attitudes then make similarly extreme candidates feel like natural allies.
  • This provides a psychological explanation for the rise of extreme politicians that complements structural explanations (like primary elections): as people increasingly define themselves by their political opinions, they demand candidates who match their own extremity.
  • Even when people know nothing about a political issue, merely framing the issue as relevant to their party's identity causes them to adopt more extreme positions and prefer more extreme candidates — extremity is driven more by identity motives than by actual knowledge.
  • Reducing the identity relevance of political issues could, in principle, reduce support for extreme candidates — a theoretically grounded intervention point that hasn't been tested before.
