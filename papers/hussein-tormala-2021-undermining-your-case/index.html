Undermining Your Case to Enhance Your Impact: A Framework for Understanding the Effects of Acts of Receptiveness in Persuasion

Mohamed A. Hussein, Zakary L. Tormala

Personality and Social Psychology Review (2021)

Abstract

Past research has uncovered actions that would seem to undermine but in fact frequently enhance persuasion. For example, expressing doubt about one's view or presenting arguments against it would seem to weaken one's case, but can sometimes promote it. We propose a framework for understanding these findings. We posit that these actions constitute acts of receptiveness — behaviors that signal openness to new information and opposing viewpoints. We review four classes of acts of receptiveness: conveying uncertainty, acknowledging mistakes, highlighting drawbacks, and asking questions. We identify conditions under which and mechanisms through which these actions boost persuasion. Acts of receptiveness appear to be more persuasive when they come from expert or high-status sources, rather than non-expert or low-status sources, and to operate through two primary mechanisms: increased involvement and enhanced source perceptions. Following a review of this work, we delineate potentially novel acts of receptiveness and outline directions for future research.

Key Findings

  • Counterintuitively, saying 'I'm not entirely sure, but...' can make you more persuasive than projecting full confidence — but this only works when you already have credibility. For a novice or low-status source, expressing uncertainty backfires.
  • Four distinct behaviors all work via the same underlying mechanism — signaling openness: (1) conveying uncertainty, (2) acknowledging past mistakes, (3) highlighting drawbacks to your own position, and (4) asking questions rather than making declarations.
  • The expert who expresses doubt triggers deeper thinking in the audience: people scrutinize the message more carefully, which amplifies persuasion when the arguments are strong (and can backfire if arguments are weak).
  • Acknowledging a past mistake increases compliance with requests, makes doctors more trusted, and makes entrepreneurs more likely to advance in pitch competitions — effects observed across medicine, business, and consumer contexts.
  • Most people intuitively believe the opposite — that certainty, citing successes, and making strong declarative statements are more persuasive. This paper documents the gap between persuasion intuitions and actual persuasion outcomes.
