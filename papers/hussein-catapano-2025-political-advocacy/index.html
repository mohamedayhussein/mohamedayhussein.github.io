When Is Political Advocacy Effective? Understanding the Goals and Actors of Political Advocacy

Mohamed A. Hussein, Rhia Catapano

Consumer Psychology Review (2025)

Abstract

Political advocacy is increasingly common. Companies routinely voice positions on political issues, consumers mobilize around issues they care about, and campaigns spend billions of dollars each election cycle. In this review, we define the construct of political advocacy, distinguish it from related constructs, and review the fast-growing interdisciplinary literature on the topic. We organize our review around a central question: when is political advocacy effective? We posit that the answer depends on two critical factors: the goal of political advocacy and the actor behind it. Drawing on decades of research across marketing, psychology, political science, management, and economics, we identify four goals that frequently underlie political advocacy: capturing attention, changing attitudes, inspiring action, and cultivating affiliation. For each goal, we review relevant findings and organize them by actor type (political campaign, company, and consumer). We conclude by discussing open questions and promising future directions for research on political advocacy.

Key Findings

  • The same political advocacy tactic can advance one goal while undermining another — for example, protests are effective at capturing media attention but tend to backfire for changing attitudes among skeptics.
  • For political campaigns, advocacy efforts work best when voters have weak prior beliefs: during primaries, down-ballot races, and ballot measure elections, not in high-profile general elections where voters are already entrenched.
  • For companies, the structural features of their business matter as much as the message: companies with less substitutable products (i.e., harder to boycott) and products consumed privately rather than publicly weather political backlash better.
  • Consumers frequently misjudge persuasion strategy — they tend to target opponents when they would have more impact persuading weak supporters who are already leaning their way but haven't taken action yet.
  • Moral reframing — presenting a political position in terms of the audience's own moral values rather than your own — consistently improves persuasion, especially across ideological divides, and works for campaigns, companies, and consumers alike.
