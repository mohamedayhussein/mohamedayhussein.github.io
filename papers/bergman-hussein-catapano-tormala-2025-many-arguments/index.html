Fifteen Reasons You Should Read This Paper: How Providing Many Arguments Increases Perceptions of Both Expertise and Persuasive Intent

Abby Bergman, Mohamed A. Hussein, Rhia Catapano, Zakary L. Tormala

Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin (2025)

Abstract

People generally believe that more is better in persuasion, for good reason. Past research has shown that providing more arguments can enhance a message's persuasiveness. In contrast, we demonstrate that increasing the number of arguments in a message can have conflicting effects on perceptions of the message source. Compared to using few arguments, using many arguments makes the source seem more like an expert, increasing persuasion, but it can also make the source appear to have greater persuasive intent, decreasing persuasion. These perceptions suppress each other, resulting in minimal or no overall benefit to persuasion. We document these effects across multiple experiments. We further demonstrate that providing many arguments can have a clear positive or negative effect, depending on whether high expertise or low persuasive intent is more valued. These findings expand our understanding of argument quantity effects in persuasion and contribute to a growing literature on conflicting source perceptions.

Key Findings

  • The common belief that 'more arguments = more persuasion' is wrong in many real-world contexts: piling on arguments can actually leave you no better off than giving just one or two.
  • When someone provides many arguments, audiences draw two conflicting inferences simultaneously — they see the person as more knowledgeable (which helps persuasion) but also as more intent on manipulating them (which hurts persuasion). These two effects cancel each other out.
  • Whether many arguments help or hurt depends heavily on context. In settings where expertise is prized (e.g., court testimony, medical advice), more arguments help. In settings where people are wary of being sold to (e.g., marketing, politics), more arguments can backfire.
  • Most laypeople (59%) incorrectly assume that giving more arguments is always the better persuasion strategy, meaning persuaders are systematically miscalibrating how much to say.
  • The persuasive intent effect is a new finding: prior research focused on weak arguments undermining persuasion, but this paper shows that even strong arguments in large quantities can trigger suspicion about motives.
